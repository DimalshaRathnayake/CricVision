Research Domain

Domain, gap, problem, objectives and methodology

Automatic cricket video segmentation and retrieval through multimodal AI, scoreboard OCR, umpire-signal event detection, player identification, timestamp synchronization and structured search.

Literature Survey

Sports video analysis has developed from traditional rule-based systems into deep learning and multimodal video understanding approaches. In cricket, researchers have studied event detection, video summarization, highlight generation, scoreboard automation, umpire-signal recognition, semantic event classification and player-specific highlight generation [1], [2], [5], [6], [7], [8].

Existing cricket video summarization systems help users reduce the time required to watch long match recordings. However, many of these systems mainly generate highlight clips or summarized videos rather than creating structured player-event records. This limits their ability to support direct retrieval tasks such as finding all boundaries by a batsman, locating a specific wicket, or identifying wickets taken by a particular bowler [1], [2], [6], [15].

Umpire-signal recognition has also been explored as a method for detecting cricket events. Earlier and recent studies have used machine learning, CNN-based models, deep features, SVM classifiers and temporal models to identify umpire actions such as SIX, OUT, WIDE and NO BALL [20], [21], [22], [23], [24], [25]. These studies confirm that umpire gestures are useful visual cues, but most of them focus on event recognition rather than linking those events with batsmen, bowlers and timestamps.

Multimodal systems combine visual, audio and textual signals for cricket video analysis. OCR-based scoreboard extraction can provide player names and match context, while computer vision models can detect visual event evidence such as umpire gestures. Recent work also shows that OCR, action recognition, transfer learning and deep learning-based event spotting can improve sports video understanding [13], [14], [18], [27], [28], [30], [31].

Research Gap

Although existing studies have made progress in cricket event detection, highlight generation, scoreboard automation and video summarization, they do not fully address the need for an integrated cricket-specific retrieval pipeline. Existing approaches commonly detect events or generate summaries, but they do not consistently connect event type, player identity, player role, timestamp and playable video segment in a searchable dashboard.

The main gap is the lack of a complete workflow that combines scoreboard OCR, umpire-signal event recognition, NER-supported player identification, batsman and bowler role extraction, timestamp synchronization, video segmentation and query-based retrieval. As a result, users still need to manually review long cricket videos to locate player-specific actions.

Traditional highlight systems also do not adequately support player-wise filtering, event-wise navigation, wicket attribution or structured search queries such as “show the first six”, “show wickets”, “Kohli batting”, “Kohli out” or “wickets by Bumrah”.

Research Problem

Cricket videos are long and contain many important moments such as boundaries, wickets, appeals, scoreboard changes and player-specific actions. Fans, coaches, analysts, broadcasters and training institutions often need to locate specific moments quickly. However, without an automated player-event retrieval system, they must manually scroll through long recordings.

This manual review process is time-consuming, inconsistent and inefficient. Existing highlight clips may show only selected moments and may not preserve complete player-event context. Therefore, there is a need for a system that can automatically identify events, extract player identities, associate those players with events and provide timestamped video retrieval.

The research problem addressed in this project is the absence of a practical cricket-specific system that jointly performs event detection, player identification, event-player association, timestamp synchronization, video segmentation and dashboard-based retrieval.

Research Objectives

  • Develop an automated cricket video segmentation framework based on player identity and event type.
  • Detect major cricket events such as FOUR, SIX, OUT and NO-ACTION using umpire-signal recognition.
  • Extract scoreboard information using OCR and identify batsmen and bowlers from noisy broadcast text.
  • Apply NER-supported and dictionary-based fuzzy matching for player-name recognition.
  • Synchronize detected events and player information using timestamps.
  • Generate structured event-player records containing event type, player role and timestamp.
  • Support player-wise filtering, event-wise navigation, wicket attribution and query-based retrieval.
  • Reduce manual video review effort for cricket fans, coaches, analysts and broadcasters.
Methodology

Multimodal cricket video intelligence pipeline

The proposed framework converts unstructured cricket videos into searchable, timestamped event-player records using visual and textual processing streams.

01

Video Upload and Preprocessing

Cricket videos are uploaded to the system and processed at selected frame intervals. Each extracted frame is stored with its frame index and timestamp. This timestamp is later used to connect detected events with the correct video position.

02

Scoreboard Region Extraction

Since cricket scoreboards usually appear near the bottom of broadcast frames, the lower region of each frame is cropped and passed to the OCR pipeline. This reduces background noise and helps the OCR model focus on player names, scores and over information.

03

Scoreboard OCR Processing

EasyOCR is used to extract scoreboard text. The raw OCR output is cleaned by removing unnecessary symbols, filtering score values, normalizing text and extracting candidate player-name tokens. This step prepares noisy scoreboard text for player identification [13].

04

Umpire Detection

EfficientNet-B0 is used as a binary classifier to detect whether an umpire is visible in a frame. This step prevents the event classifier from processing irrelevant frames that only contain players, crowd, field background or scoreboard graphics [26].

05

Umpire Gesture Classification

Frames containing umpires are passed to a ResNet-18 classifier. The classifier identifies cricket event classes such as FOUR, SIX, OUT and NO-ACTION. The NO-ACTION class helps reduce false event detection when an umpire is visible but not signalling a major event [11].

06

Player Identification

Player names are identified using OCR candidate extraction, NER-supported entity detection and fuzzy dictionary-based matching. This improves robustness when OCR produces partial names, spelling errors or extra scoreboard text [12].

07

Event-Player Association

The detected event timestamp is matched with the closest available scoreboard/player information. The final event-player record contains the event type, timestamp, batsman details, bowler details and video segment reference.

08

Video Segmentation

Playable video segments are generated around detected event timestamps. Each segment is connected with event metadata so that users can directly navigate to meaningful match moments instead of manually searching through long videos.

09

Structured Retrieval

The dashboard supports cricket-specific retrieval queries such as “first six”, “show wickets”, “Kohli batting”, “Kohli out” and “wickets by Bumrah”. The rule-based retrieval layer searches structured event-player records and returns the matching timestamped segment.

Dataset and Annotation

The datasets were prepared from more than 100 publicly available cricket videos collected from YouTube. These videos included different teams, broadcast styles, scoreboard layouts, resolutions, lighting conditions and camera angles.

  • Umpire detection dataset: 1084 training images and 270 testing images.
  • Umpire detection classes: umpire and non-umpire.
  • Umpire pose dataset: 2000 labelled images.
  • Pose classification classes: FOUR, SIX, OUT and NO-ACTION.
  • Pose dataset split: 1600 training images and 400 testing images.
  • Scoreboard OCR evaluation dataset: 3800 manually verified records.
  • Player role-label evaluation: 800 manually verified role-label instances.

Implemented Technologies

The system was implemented as a Python-based video analysis and retrieval pipeline. Computer vision, OCR, deep learning, text processing and structured retrieval methods were combined to produce a complete cricket video intelligence workflow.

  • OpenCV for frame extraction and video processing.
  • EasyOCR for scoreboard text extraction.
  • PyTorch, timm and torchvision for model inference.
  • EfficientNet-B0 for umpire presence detection.
  • ResNet-18 for umpire gesture classification.
  • Transformer-based NER for player-name extraction.
  • Fuzzy string matching for player roster matching.
  • MiniLM embeddings and FAISS-style indexing for semantic retrieval support.
  • HTML, CSS and JavaScript for the research website interface.

Expected Outcome

The final outcome of this research is a cricket video segmentation and retrieval framework that reduces manual review effort by automatically producing timestamped, searchable and player-linked match records.

The dashboard allows users to retrieve match moments based on player identity, event type, wicket context and cricket-specific query patterns. This makes the system useful for cricket coaching, tactical review, sports analytics, broadcasting support and fan-based content navigation.

Limitations and Future Work

The current implementation focuses on FOUR, SIX, OUT and NO-ACTION event classes. Other cricket events such as wides, no-balls, run-outs, reviews, appeals and near-miss wicket events can be added in future versions.

OCR performance can be affected by motion blur, low resolution, scoreboard style changes and partial occlusion. Future work can improve robustness using stronger OCR preprocessing, temporal video models, larger datasets and more advanced player-entity linking.
